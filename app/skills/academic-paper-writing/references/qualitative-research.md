# Qualitative Research

Use this file when the manuscript reports interviews, focus groups, ethnography, participant observation, qualitative case study, grounded theory, thematic analysis, discourse analysis, content analysis, framework analysis, or other qualitative coding and interpretation.

Do not force qualitative research into a quantitative methods template. The reader needs enough context to understand how data were generated, interpreted, and bounded.

## Intake questions

Before drafting or revising, identify:

- research paradigm or analytic approach
- study setting and context
- participant recruitment and sampling strategy
- inclusion and exclusion logic
- researcher role, positionality, or reflexivity issues
- data collection method: interview, focus group, observation, documents, artifacts
- interview guide, focus group guide, observation protocol, or coding protocol
- language of data collection and translation process if relevant
- coding and analytic process
- how themes, categories, or interpretations were developed
- credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability strategies where appropriate
- representative quotations or examples
- limits of transferability

## Methods checklist

Make the methods section transparent about:

- why a qualitative design fits the research question
- recruitment site, sampling method, and sample rationale
- participant characteristics relevant to interpretation
- data collection setting and mode
- who collected the data and their relationship to participants
- whether a guide or protocol was piloted or revised
- recording, transcription, translation, and anonymization process
- coding approach: inductive, deductive, hybrid, framework-based, line-by-line, or another method
- number and role of coders
- how disagreements or analytic differences were handled
- software use if relevant, without implying software performed interpretation
- how themes were refined and checked against the data

## Findings checklist

Qualitative findings should not read like unsupported generalizations.

Check that findings:

- name each theme or category clearly
- explain how each theme answers the research question
- include representative quotations, field notes, excerpts, or examples when available
- connect interpretation to supplied data rather than inventing participant meaning
- show variation, deviant cases, or tensions when they matter
- avoid treating frequency language as statistical evidence unless the design supports it
- keep participant confidentiality intact

## Trustworthiness language

Use the venue's preferred terminology. Depending on field and methodology, helpful terms may include:

- credibility
- transferability
- dependability
- confirmability
- reflexivity
- audit trail
- triangulation
- member checking
- peer debriefing
- thick description
- information power

Do not add these terms as decoration. Use only the strategies the user actually performed or can document.

## Reporting guideline routing

- Use COREQ when the study is mainly interviews or focus groups and the venue expects health-research style reporting.
- Use SRQR for broader qualitative health research or when COREQ is too narrow.
- Follow field-specific qualitative standards when the venue, discipline, or methodology names them.

## Common failure modes

- Methods omit the analytic approach.
- Sampling is described only as "participants were recruited" without strategy or rationale.
- Reflexivity is ignored where researcher role materially affects interpretation.
- Findings list themes but provide no supporting quotations or examples.
- Discussion overclaims transferability beyond the study context.
- Quantitative language implies prevalence without an appropriate design.
