# Section Playbooks

Use this file when drafting or revising an empirical paper, an abstract, or a section-level rewrite.

## Global structure

IMRaD is a common default for empirical academic writing, but not a universal law. Use it for many science and social science papers. Adapt it when the venue, field, or paper type calls for another structure.

Check that the paper answers these questions in order:

1. Why does this problem matter?
2. What exact question does the paper answer?
3. How was the question studied?
4. What was found?
5. What do the findings mean?

## Title

Write titles that are specific, searchable, and proportional to the claim.

- Name the key topic, population, dataset, method, or intervention when relevant.
- Avoid hype words such as "novel," "revolutionary," or "groundbreaking" unless required by a venue style.
- Do not promise causality if the study design only supports association.

## Abstract

Treat the abstract as a compressed version of the whole paper, not as a teaser.

Include, in compact form:

- problem or objective
- method or research approach
- core results
- conclusion or implication

Checks:

- Keep it consistent with the paper body.
- Report actual findings, not vague claims that results are "discussed."
- Do not introduce citations unless the venue explicitly allows them.
- Follow the venue's word limit and structured or unstructured format.

## Introduction

Move from context to gap to objective.

Build introductions around this logic:

1. Establish the broader problem or scholarly context.
2. Show what is known.
3. Show what remains unclear, contested, limited, or overlooked.
4. State the paper's objective, question, hypothesis, or contribution.
5. Briefly preview the approach if the field expects it.

For synthesis-heavy fields, make sure the introduction does more than summarize prior work. It should justify why this paper had to be written.

## Literature review inside an article

If the paper has a dedicated literature review section, do not turn it into a source-by-source list.

Instead:

- group sources by theme, method, debate, or chronology
- compare them explicitly
- identify agreement, disagreement, blind spots, or methodological limits
- close by showing how the current study responds

Read `literature-review.md` for deeper guidance.

## Methods

Write methods so that readers can evaluate the study and, where appropriate, reproduce it.

Usually include:

- design
- setting or context
- participants, corpus, archives, or dataset
- inclusion and exclusion logic
- materials or instruments
- procedures
- variables, coding scheme, or analytic framework
- statistical or interpretive methods
- ethics approval or consent details when relevant

Checks:

- Define terms before using abbreviations heavily.
- Keep the order parallel with the order later used in results.
- Do not hide key analytic decisions in vague phrases such as "standard methods were used."

## Results

Present findings in the order that best answers the research question.

- Start with the highest-priority result.
- Use tables and figures to reduce repetition, but interpret the main pattern in prose.
- Distinguish raw findings from interpretation.
- Report negative, null, or mixed results when they materially affect the conclusion.

Avoid:

- discussing broad implications too early
- repeating every table entry in sentences
- changing terminology between methods and results

## Discussion

The discussion should interpret rather than repeat.

Usually do the following:

1. Answer the research question directly.
2. Interpret the main findings.
3. Compare them with prior literature.
4. Explain theoretical, practical, or methodological implications.
5. State limitations without theatrics.
6. Suggest the next step only if it follows from the study.

Checks:

- Do not overclaim beyond the design.
- Do not hide limitations until the last sentence.
- Keep the paper's main contribution visible throughout the section.

## Conclusion

Use the conclusion to land the paper cleanly.

- Restate the main takeaway in sharper form, not copied wording.
- Tie back to the gap or question from the introduction.
- End on contribution, implication, or unresolved issue, depending on the genre.

## Humanities and theory-heavy variation

If the paper is not empirical, do not force IMRaD.

Use a thesis-driven structure instead:

- state the interpretive claim early
- define the conceptual frame
- organize sections by argumentative moves, not methods/results labels
- make each section advance the thesis with textual, historical, legal, or conceptual evidence

## Style checks

During rewriting, prefer:

- one clear paragraph function at a time
- explicit transitions between claims
- verbs that show argument: argues, shows, suggests, challenges, demonstrates
- concise phrasing over inflated nominalizations

Useful final questions:

- Can a reader identify the paper's contribution in one sentence?
- Does each section perform a distinct job?
- Are claims matched to evidence and study design?
- Would a reviewer know exactly what changed if this section were revised?
