# Literature Review

Use this file when the user is writing:

- a standalone literature review
- a review article
- the literature review section of a paper or thesis
- a synthesis-heavy introduction

## Core principle

Do not write a literature review as an annotated bibliography.

A strong literature review does four jobs at once:

1. summarize relevant scholarship
2. synthesize patterns across sources
3. evaluate strengths, weaknesses, and tensions
4. position the current paper, thesis, or research agenda

## First decide what kind of review this is

Classify the task before drafting:

- narrative review
- literature review section inside a larger paper
- standalone review article
- systematic review or scoping review

If the review is systematic or protocol-driven, also read `submission-and-integrity.md` and follow the relevant reporting guideline.

## Systematic review and scoping review mode

Do not write a systematic review, meta-analysis, or scoping review as a narrative literature review. A transparent review reports a reproducible process, not only a polished synthesis.

Before drafting or revising this type of review, verify:

- review question
- eligibility criteria
- databases and registers searched
- full search strategy for at least one database, and where the complete strategies are available
- date of last search
- screening process, including number of reviewers if applicable
- data extraction or charting process
- risk-of-bias, quality-appraisal, or critical-appraisal tool when applicable
- synthesis method: narrative synthesis, tabulation, meta-analysis, thematic synthesis, mapping, or other approach
- protocol registration or preregistration if applicable
- PRISMA 2020 checklist and flow diagram for systematic reviews
- PRISMA-ScR checklist for scoping reviews

When information is missing, use bracketed placeholders such as `[report full search strategy]` or ask for the missing protocol detail. Do not invent databases searched, screening numbers, excluded-study counts, risk-of-bias judgments, PROSPERO IDs, or effect estimates.

Systematic reviews usually need enough methodological detail for another team to understand how the evidence set was assembled. Scoping reviews usually need clear explanation of the scope, charting process, and how the map of evidence supports the stated purpose.

## Build the review around a guiding question

Anchor the review in one of these:

- the paper's research question
- an argumentative thesis
- a conceptual problem
- a methodological debate

If you cannot name the guiding question in one sentence, the review will likely turn into summary without synthesis.

## Organization options

Choose one dominant organizing logic:

- thematic
- methodological
- theoretical
- chronological
- by population, corpus, or case

Use chronology only when the time progression itself matters. Otherwise theme or debate usually produces stronger synthesis.

## Recommended structure

### Introduction

State:

- the topic and scope
- why the review matters
- the organizing principle
- any inclusion, exclusion, or search logic if relevant

### Body

For each section or subsection:

- open with the claim or organizing point
- group multiple sources together
- compare how they align or differ
- evaluate what they collectively establish
- identify what remains unresolved

### Conclusion

Close with:

- the current state of the field
- the main gaps, disputes, or limits
- how the user's project responds or where the field should go next

## Paragraph pattern

Use paragraphs that sound like synthesis, not note-taking:

1. Topic sentence with a claim about the literature.
2. Evidence from several sources.
3. Comparison or tension across those sources.
4. Evaluation or implication.
5. Link back to the user's paper or the next subsection.

## Discipline-sensitive guidance

- Humanities reviews often require more explicit interpretation and theoretical positioning.
- Science and social science reviews more often compare study designs, datasets, and results.
- In thesis chapters, a literature review may need to justify both the research gap and the methodological choice.

## Common failure modes

Avoid:

- one paragraph per source
- summary without comparison
- excessive quotation
- vague claims such as "many studies have examined..."
- listing gaps without explaining why they matter
- treating all sources as equally strong
- calling a review "systematic" without reproducible search, screening, extraction, and synthesis methods
- using a PRISMA flow diagram or checklist as decoration when the underlying process was not systematic

## Revision checklist

Ask:

- What is the exact question or debate this review organizes?
- Does each subsection advance that question?
- Are the most important sources synthesized instead of merely mentioned?
- Are strengths, limits, contradictions, and omissions made explicit?
- Does the review clearly create a place for the user's study or argument?
- If this is a systematic or scoping review, are the review question, eligibility criteria, search, screening, extraction/charting, appraisal, synthesis, and protocol information explicit?
- If this is a narrative review, is it labeled honestly rather than made to look systematic without the methods?
